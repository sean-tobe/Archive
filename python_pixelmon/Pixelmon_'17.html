Pixelmon

At first I wanted to implement a game that featured two visual perspectives (sketched below on yellow paper), a play on the perception of the first, second, and third dimension. I was later inspired by viewing old gameplay of Pokemon Red/Ruby and the game’s implementation of 2-D sprites, map layout, and narrative. This virtual world was confined to a 2-D plane and the player viewed the game from an aerial perspective, however, I wanted to implement a first person point-of-view of this flat world (so the player sees in one dimension). My concept challenges a conventional virtual environment by spawning the player with a minimal user interface, but including progressional interaction such as NPC dialogue. Later into the game, the player will then experience a classic 2-D experience to contrast with the 1-D experience. In reference to Edwin Abott Abott’s “Flatland” (1884), A Square is a two-dimensional square plane that lives in flat land society and is visited by A Sphere from the higher third-dimensional world to learn infinitely more exists around them. A Square and all other 2-D beings perceive the world on the 2-D plane world, so their eye only see lets them see the point in front of them. These beings must learn to see in color, understand depth perception, or simply feel their terrain. In “1-D-VENTURE”, the player Red Square spawns and the user can only see what the 2-D square would be viewing on the screen, pure magenta- its mother talking. There is a physical terrain to the map, including dirt, grass, trees, buildings, and entities, but the screen only displays the color of the block the player is standing on or near. The player must be questioning if anything exists in this world, but the player must now create a positional mental map and slowly examine how to progress. “When you play a videogame you enter into the world of the programmers who made it. You have to do more than identify with a character on the screen. You must act for it.”, by Sherry Turkle in “Video Games and Holding Power” (1983). Following your mother’s advice to stay on the light brown dirt path and explore, the player identifies the blocks on the map through the movements/colors in order to solve a hidden puzzle. Turkle also said, “constructed worlds are permeated with the spirit of a computer program, their constraints are those imposed by rule systems, not by physical reality or moral considerations”, so by maintaining verisimilitude, the player emphasizes for this world. This one dimensional point-of-view parody of the Pokemon game adventure allows the player to identify with a flat square and their everyday challenges, such as walking and buying food.

According to “The Six Elements and the Causal Relations Among Them” by Brenda Laurel, Aristotle’s six fundamentals of play drama can be translated to human-computer interactions in games. I’ve implemented story progression in  “1-D-VENTURE” to include ideas of language, character, inner thought, and various sensory dimensions. For example, later into the game the player discovers that they live in 2-D and can now view the world from an aerial perspective.

The visual enactments on the display help narrate the game’s “thought” and stimulate the player’s cognition and emotions. This surprise enables the player to visually check the algorithm they established with the game. In reference to Lev Manovich’s “Database as a Symbolic Form” (1999), as the player levels up to learn the hidden logic and algorithm of the game, they are establishing a mental model of the computational model.

If Red Square is curious if more exists, the mysterious yellow square can be found in the next level where it reveals itself to be a being from an even higher dimension, the third-dimension.

      It explains the purpose of the game, how a 2-D being perceives a point and a 3-D being perceives a plane. I implemented this game though Python and Pygame, utilizing a 2-D array of characters to format the map layout. Player action is controlled through the arrow keys and spacebar. The 1-D view is implemented by displaying a rectangle of color the player is currently colliding with.

Also shown is my implementation of dialogue between player and map entities. I’ve included map updates relative to the player’s game progression, such as your neighbor can be found on level 2 asking you, “see anything cool yet?”.
